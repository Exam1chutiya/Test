SECTION A – Memory Based Questions (2 marks each)

Q1. Define deep learning and its relation to neural networks.

Deep learning is a subset of machine learning that uses multi-layered artificial neural networks to automatically learn hierarchical feature representations from data. It mimics the structure of the human brain by organizing neurons in layers (input, hidden, output). Neural networks are the backbone of deep learning, and when these networks are deep (many layers), they enable extraction of complex patterns in images, audio, and text.

Q2. What is the purpose of data preprocessing in healthcare AI applications?

Data preprocessing ensures that medical data is clean, consistent, and usable by AI models. It includes noise removal, missing value handling, normalization, anonymization, and harmonizing formats. This improves model performance, reliability, and safety in clinical decision-making.

Q3. State one limitation of black-box AI models in clinical settings.

Black-box models lack interpretability, meaning clinicians cannot easily understand how predictions are made. This reduces trust and makes it difficult to verify correctness in critical healthcare scenarios.

SECTION B – Concept Based Questions (4–6 marks)

Q6. Discuss the role of interpretability in building trustworthy AI systems.

Interpretability ensures that clinicians can understand how AI models derive predictions. It helps verify correctness, detect bias, support clinical reasoning, and improve accountability. Transparent models increase trust, regulatory compliance, and adoption in real-world healthcare workflows.

Q7. Describe challenges of implementing NLP in multilingual healthcare datasets.

Challenges include limited annotated datasets across languages, variations in medical terminology, transliteration complexity, code-mixing in clinical notes, ambiguity, and inconsistent grammar structures. Domain-specific abbreviations further complicate entity recognition and text classification tasks.

Q8. Explain how wearable IoT devices generate data for predictive analytics.

Wearable devices collect continuous physiological signals such as heart rate, blood oxygen, gait, and sleep patterns. This real-time data is transmitted to cloud systems, where AI models analyze trends to predict conditions such as arrhythmia, hypertension, or early signs of chronic disease.

Q9. How does AI assist clinical decision-making? Provide an example.

AI supports decision-making by analyzing large medical datasets, identifying patterns, and providing evidence-based recommendations. For example, AI-enabled radiology systems highlight potential tumors in CT scans, helping doctors make quicker and more accurate diagnoses.

Q10. A linear regression model predicts systolic blood pressure (Y) using BMI (X): Y = 90 + 1.5X

(a) If BMI=28, calculate predicted BP.
Predicted BP = 90 + 1.5(28) = 132 mmHg.

(b) If actual BP=135 mmHg, compute residual error.
Residual = Actual – Predicted = 135 – 132 = 3 mmHg.

(c) Suggest how adding “Age” as a second feature improves the model.
Age strongly influences blood pressure. Adding it captures variation that BMI alone cannot explain, improving accuracy by reducing unexplained variance and making the regression more clinically realistic.

SECTION C – Analytical Questions (8–10 marks)

Q11. Deep Learning–based Cancer Diagnostics and Bias Mitigation

(a) Advanced CNN Architecture
A suitable architecture is ResNet, which uses residual blocks to avoid vanishing gradients and learn deeper features. The data flows as: Convolution → Batch Normalization → ReLU → Residual Skip Connection → Pooling → Fully Connected Classification Layer. This structure enables detection of subtle lung or skin cancer features such as irregular lesion borders, density variations, and microcalcifications.

(b) Dataset Bias Effects
If 80% images come from lighter-skinned patients and 20% from darker skin tones, the CNN may misclassify lesions in darker-skinned patients due to underrepresentation. This leads to unequal false negatives, delayed diagnosis, and serious clinical harm.

SECTION D – Case Study / Application Questions (10 marks each)

Q13. Predictive Hospital Analytics using AI & Big Data (Expanded Answer)

1. Predictive Modeling Pipeline
A hospital must integrate multiple data sources such as EHRs, admission logs, lab values, demographic data, and seasonal trends. Preprocessing includes duplicate removal, standardizing formats, feature scaling, missing value imputation, and converting categorical attributes using one-hot encoding. Feature engineering involves creating variables such as patient arrival patterns, disease prevalence, and historical admission rates. Model selection may include LSTM (for temporal patterns), GRU (efficient sequence learning), Prophet (trend-based forecasting), or Random Forest (nonlinear tabular predictions).

2. Hybrid Architecture with Real-Time Ingestion
Batch data (historical records) is combined with real-time streaming from Kafka/Spark Streaming. Kafka ingests live admission events while Spark processes them in micro-batches. The hybrid architecture ensures continuous updating of predictions for departments like ICU, Pediatrics, and Cardiology. The forecasting model receives a steady flow of both historical and real-time data.

3. Handling Data Imbalance, Missing Values & Sensor Noise
Imbalance is addressed using SMOTE or class-weight adjustments. Missing values are treated using KNN imputation or clinical-rule-based filling. Sensor noise from devices (e.g., heart-rate monitors) is mitigated using smoothing filters and outlier rejection. This ensures stability and clinical validity of predictions.

4. Ensuring Fairness & Avoiding Group Bias
Fairness involves ensuring predictions do not disadvantage elderly, rural, or previously high-risk groups. Methods include demographic parity testing, subgroup accuracy comparison, and bias-corrective reweighting. The model is regularly audited for fairness before deployment, ensuring ethical and unbiased patient care.

Q14. Fair and Interpretable AI in Healthcare (Expanded Answer)

1. Data Preprocessing & Bias Detection
The first step is to clean EHR and imaging data, harmonize formats, and anonymize patient attributes. Bias detection includes analyzing label distribution across gender and rural/urban groups, identifying skewed samples, and computing metrics such as disparate impact ratio. Outliers are removed using domain rules to maintain medical relevance.

2. Model Selection with Explainability
An interpretable model such as Decision Tree, GAM, or Explainable Boosting Machine can be selected for transparency. If a black-box model (CNN/XGBoost) is necessary for accuracy, explainability tools like SHAP, LIME, and Grad-CAM are applied. SHAP quantifies feature contribution for individual predictions, LIME generates local surrogate explanations, and Grad-CAM highlights regions in CT/MRI images responsible for classification. This makes AI outputs clinically understandable.

3. Fairness Evaluation with Proper Metrics
Demographic Parity checks whether prediction rates are equal across groups. Equal Opportunity ensures equal true positive rates across demographics. False Positive Rate Parity ensures misclassification risk is shared fairly across patient groups. The system undergoes continuous fairness monitoring and rebalancing of training data to minimize predictive disparities, resulting in an equitable and trustworthy AI pipeline.
